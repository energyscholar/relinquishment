Three articles. Three prohibitions. What do they have in common?

📖 Background
The Five Fields and Their Defining Scientists

Solid-state physics / condensed matter — Robert Laughlin, Horst Störmer, and Daniel Tsui shared the 1998 Nobel Prize for discovering the fractional quantum Hall effect: when electrons are confined to two dimensions under strong magnetic fields, they form exotic collective states with fractional charge. This is the physics of the 2DEG.

Topological quantum computation — Michael Freedman won the Fields Medal (mathematics' highest honor) for work in topology, then left pure math to found Microsoft's Station Q lab. His goal: build a quantum computer using the topological properties of two-dimensional systems. He proved that braiding non-Abelian anyons can perform universal computation.

Complexity science / autocatalytic sets — Stuart Kauffman, working at the Santa Fe Institute, showed mathematically that self-sustaining networks arise spontaneously when interacting components cross a critical diversity threshold. His theory explains how life could bootstrap from non-living chemistry — or from any substrate with enough interacting parts.

Computational universality — Stephen Wolfram demonstrated that even simple computational rules can produce arbitrarily complex behavior (Wolfram's Principle of Computational Equivalence). Danny Hillis built the Connection Machine, proving that massively parallel computation — many simple processors working together — can solve problems no single processor can.

Lattice dynamics / phonon physics — Brosl Hasslacher at Los Alamos studied how vibrations propagate through crystal lattices. Phonons (quanta of vibration) are the mechanism by which quantum systems in a solid communicate with the classical electromagnetic world. This is the I/O channel.

Each researcher's career is defined by one of these fields. Freedman left pure mathematics for one of them.

✓ Solved
Five scientists whose published work spans these fields are among the few people on Earth whose expertise collectively covers the intersection. Each is a public figure. Each has published extensively in their domain. Their work is available in libraries and on the internet. The convergence of their fields is visible to anyone who reads all five literatures. Almost nobody does.
