# Plan 0089: The Hobbit in the Mirror — Early Self-Deprecation

**Status:** DRAFT — annealed with perturbation
**Auditor:** Argus, Session 43
**Parent:** Plan 0090 (Master Restructure)

---

## Concept

~250-300 words. Bruce's direct talking voice. Freshly written, NOT extracted from Steel-Man A. Between Three Possibilities and Alpha Farm. One half-page. Get in, demolish, get out.

The reader has just learned the A/B/C framework. Before hearing the story, they meet the storyteller — and the storyteller demolishes his own credibility. Paradox: this BUILDS trust. A con man wouldn't do this. A delusional person wouldn't see the pattern and name it.

## Title

"The Hobbit in the Mirror"

## Voice

Bruce talking to you directly. Rougher than Steel-Man A. Not literary — honest. "Look, I know how this sounds." Compare:
- **Steel-Man A voice:** "He is a slightly fat middle-aged man whose long-expected journey began, as these things do, with an unexpected party."
- **This chapter's voice:** "I'm a middle-aged man who read too much Tolkien as a kid and might have built his entire life around the plot of his favorite novel without noticing. You should probably know that before I tell you anything else."

## Content Beats (~250-300 words)

1. **Who I am** (~60 words) — The kind of person who writes a book like this. The Hobbit parallel, stated directly: comfortable life, stranger shows up, burden carried for twenty years.

2. **The two explanations** (~80 words) — Either I lived through something genuine, or I absorbed a story at age eight and built my life inside it. I can't rule out the second one. I've tried.

3. **The cost of naming it** (~50 words) — My confidence shifted five percent from C to A the moment I recognized the pattern. Naming it made me less sure, not more.

4. **Why I'm telling you this** (~60 words) — You should know about this before you decide what to make of the rest of the book. I am almost certainly the wrong person: unreliable, mythically contaminated, too close.

## Relationship to Steel-Man A

**Repetition is deliberate and OK.** Per Bruce: "It's not bad to use the same words. Anyone who liked that bit and kept reading gets a reward when they get to the late chapter."

- Steel-Man A keeps ALL its text. No trimming.
- This chapter may repeat key phrases ("slightly fat middle-aged man," "read too much Tolkien," "five percent from C to A").
- The echo across 200 pages is the point. Early: rough, direct, half a page. Late: literary, deep, 6,000 words with Leaf by Niggle, Bombadil, Scouring, the Mountains.
- The reader who remembers the early chapter and reaches Steel-Man A gets the reward: "Oh — this is what he was talking about. And it goes SO much deeper than I expected."

## Placement

Chapter 2 of Part I (Story). After Three Possibilities (A/B/C framework), before Alpha Farm (narrative begins).

**Why here:** The reader needs A/B/C before the "5% shift from C to A" line makes sense. And the self-demolition must come BEFORE the narrative hooks them — after the hook, they're invested and it loses its deblocker power.

## Blocker Analysis

Clears the **narrator credibility blocker** — the reader's natural "who is this person and why should I listen?" By demolishing credibility upfront, Bruce earns the reader's trust. The Dunning-Kruger structure applied to the narrator himself.

## Annealing Record

**Initial design:** ~500 words, extracted from Steel-Man A, literary voice.

**Perturbations tested:**
1. Position before Three Possibilities → REJECTED (A/B/C needed first)
2. 100-word epigraph → REJECTED (too short to land)
3. Fresh writing, not extraction → ACCEPTED (rougher, more direct, better contrast with late chapter)
4. Shorter: 250-300 words → ACCEPTED (blade, not bludgeon)
5. Different title → REJECTED ("Hobbit in the Mirror" is too good)

**Post-perturbation design:** ~250-300 words, freshly written, direct voice, same placement. Shorter and rougher than initial design. Better contrast with Steel-Man A.

**Kauffman note:** Perturbations 3 and 4 escaped the "extract and polish" local minimum. The globally better solution: write it raw and short, let the late chapter carry the literary weight.
