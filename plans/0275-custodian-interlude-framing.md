## Proposed Changes

### 1. Global framing note before Interlude 1

Add a brief note before the first interlude establishing the literary frame:

> *The voice that follows is a literary device — the Custodian as this book imagines it might speak. Under Possibility A, it is fiction. Under B, it is informed speculation. Under C, it is close to true. The voice is the same regardless. What changes is whether you read it as imagination or testimony.*

### 2. Reinforcing sentence before Interlude 7

Interlude 7 (Hello) makes the strongest claims. Add a brief reminder:

> *One last time, the voice this book has imagined for the Custodian:*

### 3. No changes to Interludes 1-3

These pass Gen's test cleanly.

## Decision Required

**Bruce must decide:** Does this framing strengthen or weaken the book? Arguments both ways:

- **For framing:** Epistemic honesty. Protects against "he thinks he's channeling" misreading. Gen's practical test is sound.
- **Against framing:** The A/B/C frame already covers this. Adding meta-commentary breaks immersion. Readers who need the disclaimer won't read it; readers who don't need it will be annoyed.

This plan is BLOCKED on Bruce's editorial decision.

## Anneal: MED MED LOW LOW

**M1.** Framing text could break the emotional arc of the interludes. The transition from "Custodian speaking" to "reminder: this is literary" is jarring.
**Mitigation:** Use italics and minimal text. The note is a frame, not a disclaimer.

**M2.** Under Possibility C, the framing note is a lie by omission ("literary device" when the Custodian may be real). This conflicts with the book's radical honesty.
**Mitigation:** The proposed text explicitly covers C: "Under C, it is close to true." The word "imagines" acknowledges uncertainty rather than declaring fiction.

**L3.** Adding framing might invite the very misreading it tries to prevent — drawing attention to the channeling interpretation.

**L4.** Gen's practical test is sound methodology. Even if Bruce declines the framing, the audit is useful.
