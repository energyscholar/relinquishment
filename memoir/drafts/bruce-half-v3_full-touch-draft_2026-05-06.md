# Two Scientists Meet on a Bus

By Bruce Stephenson and Lawrence Mysak
Edited by Genevieve Prentice (if that's ok)

A Road Scholar tour bus rolled through Costa Rica carrying retired doctors, a brain surgeon or two, business administrators, Bruce Stephenson and his mother Linda, and a flute-playing professor emeritus from McGill named Lawrence Mysak.

Lawrence and his wife Janet turned out to be the kind of people who lead singalongs on busses. On the last evening Lawrence gave an impromptu flute concert that charmed Linda. To her, he remained for a long time afterward the flute-playing math professor from McGill.

At first that was all he was: a lively presence on a bus tour, musical, sociable, and fun. Only later would Bruce understand how deep Lawrence's scientific lineage ran.

One evening, in the shadow of the Arenal volcano, the tour group was assigned to a long table off to the side of a nearly empty dining room. Janet was talking with women at the far end. Before the food came, Bruce and Lawrence seized the moment to talk.

The subject was ocean dynamics, applied mathematics, and the sort of scientific work that quickly reveals whether a conversation is ordinary or not. They wished the kitchen had been slower. Over the rest of the trip they managed, always from the margins of the tour, to accumulate about eight hours of serious conversation. Bruce wished there had been more.

One evening Bruce and Linda also sat down with Lawrence to edit a short episodic memoir he had written some years earlier, about distinguished geophysicist Bob Dietz and a 1966 oceanography conference in Moscow. Bruce saw, up close, what went into shaping a short memoir without imagining he might write one himself.

Another night, Bruce asked Lawrence to name three of his favorite papers. Lawrence obliged, perhaps wondering whether Bruce would really read them. He did, that same evening. Then he read ten more. Then he read the abstracts of nearly two hundred publications in sequence.

To Bruce, these papers were not just impressive; they were master-class work, and they gave him real intellectual pleasure. To Lawrence, Bruce's attention was startling. This was not casual curiosity. It was serious, sustained reading.

By this point Lawrence was already surprised by the depth of Bruce's knowledge and interest. He had assumed, reasonably enough, that Bruce must have had advanced training. He then learned that Bruce's formal academic credential was a Bachelor of Science degree in nuclear engineering from Reed College in Portland, Oregon.

Lawrence was not deterred by this. If anything, it sharpened his view.

“Bruce, with your background and experience, you could do a PhD in three years,” he told him.

Bruce took this seriously. But he was fifty-five, living on a farm in Oregon, and carrying family commitments that made the standard institutional path impractical. The normal route was visible. It was simply not available.

For nearly two years there was no sustained contact between Bruce and Lawrence. 

Then Robin Macomber, a friend Bruce had mentored in mathematics years earlier, brought him a mathematical theory concerning tipping points and the criticality of dynamical systems near sudden transitions. Bruce evaluated Robin's work and recognized something he had seen before. Similar mathematical patterns appeared across field after field, often without cross-citation.

Bruce followed the pattern where it led.

The result was a joint paper with Robin Macomber, posted to arXiv in January 2026. It was Bruce's first real mathematics paper.

Lawrence's earlier encouragement was no longer hypothetical. There was now actual work on the table. When Bruce was able to reconnect with Lawrence in February 2026 he could legitimately say that Lawrence had inspired the work.

Not long after reconnecting, Bruce discovered something else. The mathematics he had been tracing across fields led directly back into Lawrence's own thermohaline circulation work.

That was the hinge.

Once Lawrence could see that clearly, his response changed from encouragement to action. He reshaped the paper and helped Bruce find it a plausible home at FACETS, the official interdisciplinary journal of the Academy of Science, the largest of the three academies of the Royal Society of Canada. Lawrence was not only a Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada; he had also served as president of the Academy of Science and as a subject editor for FACETS. He knew the terrain.

Lawrence's own mentor, George Szekeres, had no PhD and only a bachelor's degree in engineering, yet became one of Australia's leading mathematicians. Through George, Lawrence belonged to an older mathematical lineage in which brilliance, recognition, and formal institutional sequence did not always arrive in the expected order.

That lineage also carried another kind of significance. George Szekeres was the first mathematician to publish with Paul Erdős, giving him an Erdős number of 1. Lawrence, through his own paper with Szekeres, held an Erdős number of 2.

## Epilogue

At one point Lawrence told Bruce that memoirs are usually written by one person. This one has two authors, and the story is still unfolding.

If the present memoir is published, it may give Bruce an Erdős number of 3. For a lay mathematician living on a farm, without standard institutional credentials, that number could open mathematical doors that might otherwise remain closed. Whether or not that happens, the point is larger than the number itself.
