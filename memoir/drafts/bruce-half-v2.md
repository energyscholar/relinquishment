# Two Scientists Meet on a Bus

*Bruce Stephenson and Lawrence Mysak*

---

In January 2024, my mother Linda and I joined a Road Scholar tour through Costa Rica. I expected retirees on a bus. The bus turned out to be full of doctors, a brain surgeon or two, and a flute-playing professor emeritus from McGill with a hundred and eighty published papers.

Lawrence Mysak and his wife Janet made the group sing — that is not a metaphor. They had the whole bus doing sing-alongs as we traveled between stops, and on the last night of the tour Lawrence gave an impromptu flute concert. The performer was also a Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada and a Member of the Order of Canada.

I remember dinner in the shadow of the Arenal volcano. The meal was slow to arrive. Janet was chatting with ladies at the far end of the table. Lawrence and I seized the window and talked ocean dynamics and mathematics until the food came, and wished the kitchen had been slower. In ten days we found perhaps eight hours for real conversation, always stolen from the margins of the tour, never enough.

Two years later, when I told Linda we were back in touch, she said: "What a lovely man." She still calls him the flute-playing math professor from McGill.

What music means to Lawrence — as art, as discipline, as a way of being in the world — is his story to tell. I can only say what it was like to listen.

[Lawrence — What does music mean to you? The flute, the orchestra, the sing-alongs — whatever you'd like to share.]

---

After our first real conversation on the tour, I asked Lawrence to name three of his favorite papers. He obliged — not, I think, expecting me to read them.

I read them that week. They were master-class work — insightful, expertly structured, a pleasure the way a well-made thing is always a pleasure. Then I read ten more, and the abstracts of all hundred and eighty, time-sequenced. One evening, Linda and I sat down with Lawrence and edited one of his memoir chapters — the story of Bob Dietz and a conference behind the Iron Curtain. We saw what goes into a memoir before we knew we'd be writing one.

I don't know how that moment was for Lawrence — when I came back having done the reading. I only know what I saw from my side: a shift from friendly conversation to something more like mentorship.

[Lawrence — What was it like when Bruce came back having read the papers? Did something change for you?]

---

Lawrence told me to go to graduate school for a mathematics degree. It was good advice. I was fifty-five, on a farm in Oregon, with commitments that made the logistics impossible.

The encouragement stayed.

For nearly two years there was no sustained contact between us. During that time, a friend I'd mentored in math years earlier, Robin Macomber, returned with mathematics he'd derived — tipping points, criticality, the dynamics of systems near sudden transitions. Evaluating Robin's work, I recognized patterns from my own cross-domain reading and searched further. The same mathematical structure appeared independently across field after field, often without cross-citation. This became a paper — THE paper — posted to arXiv in January 2026.

In February, I reconnected with Lawrence and told him he had inspired me. Then I discovered something I'd missed entirely in 2024: the mathematics I'd been tracing across domains led directly back to Lawrence's own thermohaline circulation work. The encouragement had created the path that returned to the encourager.

Lawrence reshaped the paper, and it was submitted to FACETS — the journal of the Royal Society of Canada, where Lawrence had served as editor. His quoted words appear in its pages.

On the phone in April, I told him: "Had you not encouraged me, I might not have made it over the inertial bump to get it done."

What Lawrence saw in a fifty-five-year-old farmer that made him say *go to graduate school* — I'll leave that for him to explain, if he's willing.

[Lawrence — What made you suggest graduate school? What did you see in that conversation?]

---

Preparing the paper for FACETS, I did a closer pass on Lawrence's thermohaline hysteresis work. I had missed, in 2024, what I could see by 2026: the mathematics in his lineage was the mathematics I had begun to use, independently, in another system.

Lawrence and Leon Glass ride the same bus at McGill — French horn and flute, thirty years together in the I Medici orchestra, retired at the same time. Their research, it turned out, was the same mathematics too.

I leave it to Lawrence to say what that was like from his side.

[Lawrence — What was it like to discover the mathematical connection? And what about Leon — what does it mean to play music with someone for thirty years and find your research converged on the same mathematics?]

---

Lawrence said that memoirs are usually written by one person. This one has two authors, and the story is still unfolding — his Springer memoir, four hundred and thirty-three pages with over a hundred photographs, publishes this spring. If our joint work goes well, it may one day give me an Erdős number of three. For a farmer without institutional credentials, that key opens doors I can't open otherwise. I intend to use it well.

If Lawrence would like the last word, I'll step aside.

[Lawrence — Would you like to close this? The door is open.]
