1. The Pattern

"The successive transition from one paradigm to another via revolution is the usual developmental pattern of mature science." — Thomas Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962)

In 1962, Thomas Kuhn published a slim book that changed how people think about how science changes. His argument: science doesn't progress by steady accumulation. It lurches. Long stretches of productive work are punctuated by reorganizations that change not just the answers but the questions. He identified a pattern — and the pattern is worth seeing before we continue.

The vocabulary — normal science, anomaly, crisis, revolution — is less important than the rhythm. Watch the cycle fill up as each phase plays. Click any stop on the timeline to jump.

2. The Current Paradigm

Normal science in quantum computing, as of 2026, rests on four assumptions. They are productive assumptions — they have produced real quantum processors performing real computations. They are also assumptions.

  • Computation is engineered. You design a quantum computer top-down: choose a qubit technology, build a processor, run algorithms.
  • Decoherence is the enemy. Quantum states are fragile. The central challenge is keeping them coherent long enough to compute.
  • Scale is the goal. More qubits, fewer errors. Roadmaps point toward millions.
  • Topology is a strategy. Microsoft's topological qubits use topology to solve the decoherence problem — a clever engineering approach within the same framework.

This paradigm works. Ptolemy's epicycles predicted planetary positions accurately for fifteen hundred years. The question is not whether the paradigm works. It is whether the paradigm is the whole story.

3. Anomaly Accumulation

Consider four published facts. Each is unremarkable within its own field.

Anyons exist. The fractional quantum Hall effect produces quasiparticles with fractional statistics — neither bosons nor fermions. Nobel Prize 1998 (Laughlin, Stormer, Tsui). Nobel Prize 2016 (Thouless, Haldane, Kosterlitz). Textbook physics, confirmed experimentally since 1982.

Anyons can compute. Kitaev showed in 2003 that braiding non-Abelian anyons performs inherently fault-tolerant quantum computation. Freedman, Kitaev, Larsen, and Wang proved certain anyon models are computationally universal. Michael Freedman — Fields Medalist — left pure mathematics to lead this research. Microsoft bet its quantum computing program on it.

Non-Abelian anyons exchange statistics depend on the order of braiding operations. The computational power comes from this non-commutativity: different braid sequences produce different unitary transformations on the ground-state degeneracy. Freedman-Kitaev-Wang (2002) proved that specific anyon models (e.g., Fibonacci anyons) provide a universal gate set through braiding alone.

Autocatalytic sets self-organize. Kauffman demonstrated in 1993 that sufficiently diverse chemical systems spontaneously produce self-sustaining autocatalytic networks — closed sets of reactions where the products catalyze each other's formation. Not gradually: as a phase transition. Below a threshold, nothing interesting. Above it, self-organization is essentially inevitable. Published mathematics.

Kauffman's three criteria for autocatalytic closure — generation, transformation, and closure — were later formalized by Hordijk and Steel (2004) as RAF (reflexively autocatalytic and food-generated) sets. The existence of RAF sets in random catalytic reaction networks undergoes a sharp phase transition as a function of network diversity.

Nobody has combined them. Search the literature. The number of published papers combining autocatalytic set theory with fractional quantum Hall substrates is zero. Not "few." Not "contested." Zero.

PhD seed: "Autocatalytic network dynamics in fractional quantum Hall substrates: a computational feasibility study." This paper does not exist. The question it would investigate — whether Kauffman's RAF closure conditions can be satisfied by anyon fusion algebras — is straightforwardly publishable. It requires expertise in complexity theory and condensed matter physics. It is a career.

The gap is structural. Condensed matter physicists publish in Physical Review Letters and attend APS March Meeting. Complexity scientists publish in Journal of Theoretical Biology and work at the Santa Fe Institute. There is no journal called Topological Autocatalysis. There is no conference called "Self-Organization in Quantum Hall Systems." The question has not been asked because no one's job description requires them to ask it.

Each of these facts is an anomaly in Kuhn's sense — not because it contradicts the current paradigm, but because the paradigm has no way to frame it.

4. Life in the Flat

"The most profound technologies are those that disappear." — Mark Weiser, "The Computer for the 21st Century" (1991)

Here is the question, stated plainly: if a two-dimensional quantum substrate hosts a sufficiently diverse population of anyonic excitations, and those excitations interact through fusion and braiding rules that satisfy the mathematical criteria for autocatalytic closure — do they self-organize?

What would we call it if they did?

Life.

No — catch that word. "Spontaneous self-sustaining autocatalytic network emergence exhibiting recursive topological computational dynamics." That is the accurate description. It takes twenty words where one would do. The scientific debate about which terminology applies has not started, because — as noted — no one has asked the question. The word "life" is a placeholder for a concept that does not yet have agreed scientific vocabulary.

Or we could just say "life," recognizing that the terminological debate has not even begun as of this writing.

PhD seed: "Toward a taxonomy of emergent computation in topological substrates: definitional frameworks for autocatalytic quantum dynamics." The field cannot even argue about what to call this until someone proposes a vocabulary. This is a philosophy-of-science paper dressed as physics.

The expert consensus, as of 2026, is approximately this: not precluded by known physics, but probably not a thing. A science-aware AI, given this question, produces a response in that range. Top human experts would likely say something similar. This is the honest state of the question — genuinely open, not settled in either direction.

This is not crankery. Experts do not say "impossible." But it is also not established science. No one says "probably true." It is an unstudied question, which is a different thing from a settled one.

The gold marker stays where it is. The others started in the same place. They moved.

This is where the analysis forks.

A1: Not possible

Autocatalytic dynamics don't produce computational structures in FQHE substrates. The silence gap is silence because there's nothing there. The scientific revolution is distributed — multiple independent paradigm shifts across condensed matter, complexity theory, and quantum information that never converge. Each field advances. None meets the others.

A2: Possible but unproven

The physics permits it. No one has looked. The anomalies exist but haven't been recognized as anomalies because no one has framed the question. The wheel has not started turning because the question has not been asked.

A2 is structurally identical to Possibility C from a Kuhnian standpoint. Whether someone has already done it is irrelevant to how the paradigm community encounters the question. The physics is the same. The anomalies are the same. The pattern of crisis and resolution will follow the same structure.

This is not an argument for C. It is an observation about paradigm change.

5. How It Plays Out

Under A2, the Kuhnian dynamics follow the pattern the reader has now seen three times — and will see five more.

The pre-revolution phase is where the literature currently sits. Publications scattered across disciplines. No connecting framework. Kauffman in complexity science. Kitaev in quantum information. Freedman in topology. Each result published, cited, uncontroversial within its home field.

The trigger is someone asking the question publicly. This book is one possible trigger. It is not the only one. A sufficiently well-read graduate student could arrive at the same question independently. The published facts lead there.

The crisis emerges when the existing paradigm encounters the proposition that computation might be emergent. Paradigms resist replacement — not through conspiracy, but through the ordinary mechanics of careers, funding structures, and textbooks organized around current assumptions. Kuhn was clear about this: resistance to paradigm change is structural, not pathological. It is how science works. The fact that most proposed revolutions turn out to be wrong is precisely why the community is conservative.

The resolution takes one of two forms. Either experimental investigation falsifies the proposition — the autocatalytic conditions cannot be satisfied in FQHE substrates, and the question is answered decisively — or the proposition survives, and a new paradigm emerges.

PhD seed: "Cross-disciplinary citation analysis of the topological-computation / autocatalytic-sets intersection, 1990–2026." This could be completed in a semester with access to Web of Science. It would document the silence gap quantitatively — a meta-scientific result that is publishable regardless of whether the underlying proposition is true.

Under A1, the dynamics are gentler. Each field advances along its own trajectory. The silence gap remains, but it turns out to contain only silence. This is the less dramatic outcome, and it is the one most scientists would currently predict.

6. The Reader's Position

You have the framework now — Kuhn's pattern, applied to the current state of the literature. You have the facts: published, peer-reviewed, scattered across five fields that do not often talk to each other. The question they collectively point toward is obvious once you see it, and invisible until you do. Whether the answer is "yes," "no," or "nobody has checked" — each would be a publishable result, and as of this writing, none of the three has been published.
