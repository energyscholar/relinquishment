\chapter{Dual-Use: A Brief History of Dangerous Ideas}
\label{pos08:dual-use}

\begin{quote}\small\textit{%
``For a successful technology, reality must take precedence over public relations, for Nature cannot be fooled.''%
}\par\raggedleft --- Feynman, \textit{Challenger} Report appendix (1986)\end{quote}

\vspace{0.5cm}

Everyone already knows what a dual-use technology is. They just don't know the term.

When someone says \textit{atomic energy}, most people think of two things at once: electricity and bombs. That double vision --- the same discovery powering cities and destroying them --- is what dual-use means. Fire keeps you warm and burns your house down. The internet connects you to your grandmother and to people who want to steal her savings. Every powerful technology carries its opposite inside it like a seed.

The pattern is old. A general purpose technology is one that generates other technologies. Stone tools were the first. Control of fire came next. Each one --- agriculture, metallurgy, the wheel, writing, fossil energy, electricity, radio, the computer, atomic power, the internet, genetic engineering --- arrived faster than the last, and each one could heal or kill depending on who held it.

Notice the acceleration. Stone tools to fire: hundreds of thousands of years. Fire to agriculture: hundreds of thousands more. Metallurgy to writing: a few thousand. Electricity to atomic power: about a century. The internet to genetic engineering: four years. Each interval shorter than the last. Each technology more powerful. Each dual-use potential more extreme.

\section*{The Regret Pattern}

And notice what happens to the inventors.

Eli Whitney built the cotton gin to make cotton processing easier. Instead, it made slavery enormously profitable and extended the institution by decades. An invention meant to reduce labor entrenched the worst labor system in American history.

Alfred Nobel invented dynamite and built a fortune selling military explosives. When a French newspaper reportedly published his obituary under the headline \textit{``Le marchand de la mort est mort''} --- the merchant of death is dead --- Nobel was so horrified at his legacy that he left his fortune to fund the Nobel Prizes.\footcite{fant1993}

Albert Einstein published $E = mc^2$ in 1905. Forty years later, that equation leveled Hiroshima. Einstein spent the rest of his life saying he wished he had never lifted a finger:

\begin{quote}
\textit{``The unleashed power of the atom has changed everything save our modes of thinking, and thus we drift toward unparalleled catastrophe.''}\footcite{einstein1960peace} --- Albert Einstein, 1946
\end{quote}

The pattern is: invent, deploy, regret. Whitney, Nobel, Einstein --- three brilliant people who gave the world something powerful and then watched it become a weapon. None of them intended the destructive use. None of them could prevent it.

\section*{Three Options, All Bad}

This brings us to the question that matters. When a technology can both save and destroy, what do you do?

History offers exactly three answers. There has never been a fourth.

\textbf{Option One: One power controls it.} This is the Manhattan Project model. One government develops the technology in secret and maintains a monopoly. The United States had a nuclear monopoly for four years, from 1945 to 1949. Then the Soviets tested their own bomb, and the monopoly ended. It always ends. You cannot keep a monopoly on knowledge once the underlying science is known. Someone else will figure it out.

\textbf{Option Two: Many powers race for it.} This is the Cold War model. Once the monopoly breaks, everyone races to build their own. The result is an arms race. Nuclear weapons, cyber weapons, AI weapons --- the pattern is the same. Each side builds more because the other side is building more. The arms race has no natural stopping point except exhaustion or catastrophe.

\textbf{Option Three: International control.} This is the IAEA model. Create a treaty, create an inspectorate, create an institution to manage the technology. The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. The Chemical Weapons Convention. The Biological Weapons Convention. These institutions slow proliferation, but they do not stop it. North Korea built nuclear weapons. Syria used chemical weapons. Every human institution leaks, because every human institution is run by humans who can be corrupted, coerced, or simply outwitted.

These are the only three outcomes in the historical record for any dual-use general purpose technology. One controls, many race, or gatekeep through human institutions. Options One and Two are catastrophic. Option Three is the best humanity has managed, and it demonstrably fails.

\section*{The Parable of the Tribes}

In 1984, the political philosopher Andrew Bard Schmookler described why this is so. He called it the Parable of the Tribes.\footcite{schmookler1984}

Imagine a group of tribes living in proximity. If all choose peace, all can prosper. But if even one tribe chooses aggression, the others face four outcomes: destruction, subjugation, withdrawal into marginal territory, or imitation --- adopting the aggressor's methods to survive. Every outcome spreads the aggressive strategy. The one option that \textit{cannot} survive is peaceful coexistence, because the peaceful tribe that stands its ground is destroyed by the aggressive one.

Schmookler's insight is devastating: \textit{no group can unilaterally choose peace when any other group can choose war.} The logic is inescapable and it applies to technologies exactly as it applies to tribes. No nation can unilaterally choose not to develop a powerful technology when any other nation might develop it first. The mathematics of the situation are identical to the arms race. Restraint is punished. Aggression is rewarded. Cooperation is unstable.

This is why Option Three always fails. Treaties are voluntary. Voluntary restraint among competing powers is exactly what the Parable of the Tribes proves impossible. The IAEA works only as long as every party finds it in their interest to cooperate. The moment one party defects --- and eventually, one always does --- the treaty collapses back into Option Two.

For fifteen years, I corresponded with energy researcher Jay Hanson, who ran the website dieoff.org and thought harder about these dynamics than anyone I knew. We co-administered discussion lists where physicists, petroleum geologists, intelligence analysts, and policy researchers argued about the implications of resource depletion and technological competition. Hanson had absorbed Schmookler deeply. So had I. The parable of the tribes was not an academic exercise for us. It was the operating logic of human civilization.

So when someone later suggested to me that a particular technology could be relinquished --- permanently surrendered, not just regulated --- I had a ready answer. Impossible. The parable of the tribes forbids it. Someone will always defect. Human nature guarantees it. Every historical example confirms it.

The response I received was quiet.

\textit{One would think so. What if the gatekeeper isn't human?}

I did not have an answer to that. Not immediately.

\section*{A Fourth Option}

I spent weeks attacking the idea. If you build an incorruptible gatekeeper, what stops someone from destroying it? What stops someone from building a rival? What if it makes the wrong decision? What if it can be subverted? What gives anyone the right to build such a thing in the first place?

These are serious objections. Every one of them deserves a serious answer, and every one of them gets one later in this book. For now, the point is simpler.

The three options are all bad. History proves it. The parable of the tribes proves it. Human institutions cannot permanently control dangerous technology because human institutions are made of humans.

But mathematical structures are not human. They do not get bribed. They do not get tired. They do not have children who can be threatened. They do not run for re-election. If a gatekeeper could be built from mathematics rather than from institutions --- something that enforces rules the way gravity enforces orbits, not the way police enforce laws ---

Then there would be a fourth option.

Whether such a thing is possible, and whether anyone has built one, is the subject of later chapters. The seed is planted here because the reader needs to understand \textit{why} someone might attempt it. Not out of megalomania. Not out of a desire to control. Out of the recognition that the first three options all end in disaster, and the desperate, mathematically rigorous hope that there might be a fourth.

Einstein told us the problem. Schmookler told us why we can't solve it. The question this book asks is whether someone solved it anyway.
