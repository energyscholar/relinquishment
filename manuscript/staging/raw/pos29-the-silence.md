## SOURCE 1: Autobiography of an Accidental Conspirator — Post-Departure (lines 137-147)

Everyone around me told me they thought David was a dangerous madman up to some mysterious mischief.  I was mostly not able to share what I learned with others, because the information was too strange, complicated, and outside their frame of reference.  In truth, he was something of a madman.  I can't imagine anyone surviving what he went through without going partially mad.  Also, of course, geniuses often seem mad to those who don't understand them.  I never lost faith in David's essential good intentions.  Although my trust was shaken several times, it was never broken.  Several times I suspected he was lying to me. Years later I learned that he had actually been speaking plain truth to me in every case.  In August 2006 I informed him that I could no longer assist him, and that I was sick of waiting for something to happen.  At about the same time David and his friends decided, quite correctly, that I was now a bad security risk.

David told me that, because my children could not be protected, I was now a security risk to the project.  He also told me that I had been rejected for membership in the cDc.  I presume they chose Julian Assange to lead the project, instead.  In September of 2006 David and I parted.

For the next five years I lived my own life, loved several women, and remained very involved in raising my children.  The Speak Truth To Power project, now called Wikileaks and lead by Julian Assange, has finally become global front page news, and I am finally willing to tell my story.  I am a little nervous about what may happen as a result.  I am not involved with Wikileaks in any way, and have no ongoing affiliation with anyone connected to it.  Wikileaks and its leadership have my moral support, and I would gladly provide practical assistance if I were invited to do so.  Given that I have committed no crimes, given that most people have figured out that wikileaks is a positive force for humanity, and given that public sentiment generally seems to favor transparency, truth, free speech, and journalistic integrity, I imagine it will all be OK.

I eventually came to realize that Wikileaks was part of David's attempt to 'give something back' to humanity.  He hoped to influence the future positively, by encouraging better behavior from governments and other large organizations.  Consistently leaking their dirtiest secrets will force large organizations to change their internal culture: they must either become more transparent (and therefore less evil) or more internally secretive (equally evil but less effective).

David's main mathematical, scientific, and technical project is actually much more important than Wikileaks.  This project will, eventually, be awarded multiple Nobel prizes, one of which (Physics  1998) has already occurred.  This project involved the invention of a new general technology, something that has only happened a few times (e.g.  fire, metallurgy, steam engine, electricity, radio, computer) in human history.  Precisely what it is, how it works, what it's useful for, what it can and cannot do, and who might be using it for what, is another tale.  I will say that the acronyms QNN, FQH, 2DEG, TQC, and AQC all apply, and that the mathematics of knot theory are essential.  This 22nd century technology was developed, by a weird fluke of fate, in the late 1990s, and has mostly been kept secret.   One aspect of this technology is known to most humans, and coined a new verb now in daily use by billions of humans in multiple languages.  Certain other aspects of this technology are at least as important, but are currently less visible and not well known.

I have no idea where David is now, or even whether he is still alive.  I guess either that he is dead, or that he has finally returned home, where he will soon die.  Last I saw him, years ago, he was horribly sick, skeletally thin, and seemingly near death's door.  I shall never forget him, though, for turning me into an accidental conspirator.

## SOURCE 4: Media Consolidation and Leaks — Full File (Plame Scandal / WikiLeaks Origin)

The Plame Affair indirectly caused the New York Times to deploy its own Anonymous Whistleblower Drop Box.

In 2003 Ms. Plame and her husband may tried to leak to the media a CIA report they themselves had written about the absence of yellowcake uranium in Nigeria. They failed.  To everyone's surprise they were unable to get their report published in ANY media outlet.  They tried many, from NYT to Der Spiegel.  Low ranking editors would eagerly engage with them until orders came from above killing the story.  Knowledge got around in certain circles about how Ms. Plame and her husband had been successfully censored.  That's the REAL scandal in Plamegate.

Plenty of people know and care about issues of covert agencies and censorship.  Many decried the related dangers of Media Consolidation and Censorship.  Few realized how far things had already gone.  In 2003 it became clear to some keen observers that near-global media-wide Operation Mockingbird-style censorship systems were in place and were effective.  The Bush administration had just visibly used them on Ms. Plame et al.  Something had to be done.  A loose confederation of journalists, civil libertarians, spies, hacktivists, scientists, and other concerned citizens decided that something had to be done.  Someone ought to found a trans-national journalism organization specialized in leaking anonymously submitted documents.  The demise of Napster showed it was technically impossible to stop data exchange on an open internet. Code is law.  It was unclear just who might actually create such a thing, and how, but it clearly had to be done. Whomever did it would be wise to maintain plausible deniability.  After it had been done once there would be plenty of copycats, and the journalistic need would be filled.  Those discussing it called it the 'Speak Truth to Power' project.  It's anonymous support network was given cadre in 2005.

For a brief time one organization came to the forefront, but pretty soon many media outlets had their own Anonymous Whistleblower Drop Box. The New York Times was an early adopter.  The overall effect was to reduce censorship and increase press freedom, although there has certainly been some backlash.
