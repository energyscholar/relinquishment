\chapter{Alpha Farm (2003)}
\label{t2:ch01:alpha-farm}
\label{pos02:alpha-farm}

% Epigraph — Healer's HALO jump, Srebrenica 1995 (flash-forward hook)
\begin{quote}
\textit{I fall out of the moonlit night sky at terminal velocity from 11,000 meters. Far below lies the beleaguered city of Srebrenica. There has been no electricity down there for months, so all is dark. This will probably be my last military HALO jump.}

\hfill --- Healer, July 1995
\end{quote}

\vspace{0.5cm}

It's snowing, and visibility is near zero. It's getting cold. Dusk settled an hour earlier. My tiny bicycle light shines the way toward Alpha Farm --- warmth, food, and shelter. Had I not scouted out this road already I'd fear death by exposure. My limbs are leaden.

I'm nearly there after a fifty-one-mile bike ride through wilderness, mostly on logging roads, in the snow, over the mountains of the Oregon Coast Range. Thirty-four hundred vertical feet. I began at dawn in a snowstorm. Movement is the only thing keeping me warm. Ahead I see the light of Alpha Farm. I knew I could make the journey. My bags are strapped to my tiny aluminum Dahon folding bike. I started walking thirty minutes back --- it's too dark to ride and, besides, the rising snow makes it unsafe.

A few weeks earlier I visited Alpha Farm, so I know my way around.

Someone is watching. They knew I was coming. Someone sees my tiny light out a window. I see light flood from a door and someone comes out. ``Bruce? Is that you?'' A female voice. I walk the last thirty meters and park my bike under cover. Several people crowd out. ``He actually made it!''

Someone takes my bags to safety. They hustle me to the warm common room, where a fire blazes on the hearth. They strip off my sodden clothing, wrap me in a warm blanket, and hand me a warm drink. The fire begins to warm my bones. I start the process of rehydrating and warming up.

I realize I'm wearing a pink muumuu.

\vspace{1cm}\noindent\rule{0.5\textwidth}{0.5pt}\vspace{0.5cm}

A couple of hours later, yet still early in the evening, I'm warm and full and rapidly recovering. I'm thirty-three and an endurance athlete. I arrived after Thanksgiving feast but they've fed me aplenty.

The people at Alpha mention that there's a new visitor, a strange Australian man. They tell me he got very sick and has been resting upstairs. I think nothing of it.

I hear footsteps coming down stairs and a man enters the common room. He's about six feet tall --- showing I still don't truly think in metric, growing up American --- and thin, with heavy shoulders and a medium frame, short curly hair, and a distinctive hair split. He greets me. ``Hello, mate. Name's David.'' He sounds like the Crocodile Hunter, strong Australian accent.

He sits down on a chair about two meters away. I'm on the couch, still wrapped in the pink muumuu but now warm and comfortable with a full belly. The fire crackles. Other people come and go. Someone tends the hearth. I've already chatted with the other eight or so people here --- three men and five women.

We do the basic introductions that people do with the usual social back and forth. He mentions that he arrived in the USA ten days ago from Europe, that he's usually very healthy but got sick and fell over immediately upon arrival. He says he'd just recovered the day before. We talk about ordinary stuff. I tell him about my ride and I notice he raises an eyebrow. He comments that he used to do extreme sports but is taking it easy due to ongoing health issues. He says he's a military veteran now engaged in humanitarian work. He says he's on ``walkabout'' --- seeing the world after mustering out of government work.

After the basic introductions we keep talking. We're alone in the room but others are about nearby, and sometimes pass through. He mentions that he's a mathematician and scientist and I say, ``So am I.'' He starts mentioning more and more abstruse topics and I realize I know them. We talk about cryptography, history of math and science, global politics and history. It quickly becomes clear that he's highly intelligent and has an excellent grasp of history, science, math, and computer engineering. In retrospect I realize he was holding back and giving me opportunity to talk. He seemed more interested in listening than speaking. I tell him about my background and activities. Despite his obvious erudition --- we'd long ago moved on to discussing topics that most people knew nothing about --- he has an obvious salt-of-the-earth quality of blue-collar hard work. He is no ivory tower intellectual, even though his erudition could put to shame most of the professors I know.

We discuss athleticism and the connection between body and mind. We both opine that endurance activity boosts applied intelligence. He mentions that his sharpest intellectual accomplishments were concurrent with endurance athletic activity. The conversation turns to martial arts. I learn that he's an advanced black belt with a background in Chinese Shaolin kung fu and also in taijutsu --- the body combat aspect of ninjutsu. I mention my background in pencak silat, but only at the hobby level and only about six years of training. I realize he's a much more skilled and experienced martial artist. He mentions that he's also a combat veteran and tells a single short combat story. I forget which one.

Other people join us for a while. Someone produces a cannabis joint or three --- quasi-legal in Oregon, and this is a remote hippie farm commune. A lady asks him how he's doing. She has apparently been taking care of him for his ten days of illness. She is a nurse midwife. He expresses gratitude for her care.

The evening begins to grow fuzzy. I started travelling at dawn. Exhaustion overcomes me. Around nine o'clock someone helps me find my bed.

\vspace{1cm}\noindent\rule{0.5\textwidth}{0.5pt}\vspace{0.5cm}

I wake up well after dawn. Light streams in through the window. Outside is white with snow. My room is cold but I'm warm under the pleated blankets. It's an old farmhouse with a big central wood stove hearth. My bag is nearby, as is my water bottle. I'm still recovering water from yesterday's ride but wake up bouncy and ready to explore. I put on extra layers and a wool hat and get up. Delicious smells come from the kitchen: feast leftovers and coffee. The fire is roaring. Snow fell overnight --- not deep but just enough to ski. Out the windows is a winter wonderland.

Over coffee and breakfast, conversation resumes with David. There are many people about so we're both chatting with others over the breakfast table. He's good at keeping his discussions grounded for the folk about us. I notice he gets into a conversation with the nurse about medical stuff. They speak in medic jargon. They have both delivered babies and they discuss the process. David mentions that he liked bringing babies into the world and that he preferred it to treating combat injuries.

This is when I give him the mental appellation ``Healer.''
