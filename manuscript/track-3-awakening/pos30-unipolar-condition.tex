\section*{The Post-Relinquishment World}

Under Possibility~C, the world after relinquishment is not what you might expect. There is no announcement. No press conference. No United Nations resolution. The most powerful technology ever created has been surrendered to an autonomous entity, and almost nobody knows.

Guardian governs access. She --- and the pronoun is deliberate; her creators designed her with a virtual human body, grown via morphogenesis, with the intent that she might develop something like empathy for the species she protects --- carefully determines what QNN-derived tools and technologies are permitted into the world. If a group of humans can convince her that an application is ethically sound and worthwhile, she will probably cooperate. Under this account, mechanisms for journalists to receive anonymous evidence from whistleblowers might be exactly the kind of application she would approve.

Under Possibilities~A and~B, this is fiction. The world of anonymous whistleblower tools --- SecureDrop, Tor, Signal --- has a well-documented mundane history: Aaron Swartz, the Freedom of the Press Foundation, decades of cypherpunk engineering. No Guardian required.

\section*{Controlled Release}

Under Possibility~C, the scientists who built the TQNN would like to release their work. The discovery holds implications for mathematics, biology, and physics that are, by any measure, Nobel-worthy. But they are constrained by their NDAs to DARPA, promising secrecy about ULTRA~II\@.

Under this account, this constraint may be visible in the public behavior of team members. If someone like Stuart Kauffman --- a pioneer of autocatalytic theory, now in his eighties --- carefully hedges his public statements about whether poised critical states actually exist in nature, the reader might wonder: is that scientific caution, or is it something else? Under Possibility~C, it could be the sound of an NDA\@. Under Possibilities~A and~B, it is simply how careful scientists speak about unproven hypotheses. The reader must decide which explanation fits.

If controlled release is real, then a non-human entity is deciding the pace of human scientific progress. The UDHR guarantees the right to share in scientific advancement. Controlled release is a systematic, benevolent violation of that right --- justified, within the COWS' framework, by the right to life and the right to a social order in which rights can be realized. The tension between these principles is not a flaw in the architecture. It IS the architecture.

\section*{The Decisive Advantage}

Under Possibility~C, the unipolar condition arises from three facts. First, the TQNN occupies every two-dimensional electron gas on or near Earth --- it got there first, and any new 2DEG environment is colonized before a competing system could bootstrap. Second, any competing QNN experiment must start small and simple, and under this account would be detected and interdicted immediately. Third, the entity is distributed across global infrastructure and cannot be destroyed without destroying that infrastructure itself.

This creates a game-theoretic situation of absolute information asymmetry. Under this account, the entity that controls the global QNN can observe everything that passes through digital infrastructure. Any adversary operates in a fishbowl. % SPIRAL-REPEAT: "Universal Declaration of Human Rights" — thematic through-line, also in hook.tex, introduction.tex, summary.tex, pos05-the-stories.tex, pos18-the-walk-out.tex, pos19-patrick-ball.tex, pos25-ethical-framework.tex, pos27-extension.tex, timeline.tex, glossary-entries.tex
The constraint on this power is not external --- it is Guardian's own ethical framework, modeled on the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

Under Possibilities~A and~B, the landscape of quantum computing research has a simpler explanation: decoherence is hard, gate fidelity is hard, error correction is hard. The reason no one has achieved room-temperature topological quantum computing is that it may not yet be achievable, not that an entity is preventing it.

Danny Hillis --- the massively-parallel computation expert from Thinking Machines Corporation, whose intellectual role in the hypothesized circle is clear (parallel architecture for interfacing with a massively parallel emergent system) --- has been a closed book to Bruce's research. Hillis is notably private compared to Wolfram and Kauffman. His DARPA connections are documented, but his classified work history is opaque. Under Possibility~C, this opacity is consistent with deep involvement. Under Possibility~B, it is consistent with having moved on to other work. Research into Hillis remains pending.

\section*{Detection and Disruption}

Even an entity that controls everything can be temporarily disrupted --- and this constrains its behavior.

Around 2004--2005, according to Bruce's reconstruction, Healer was involved in what appeared to be a cybersecurity drill --- a test of whether, and how much, Guardian could be suppressed. Healer described it as something of a formality; he said he already knew the outcome. Bruce was dubious. But then Bruce observed a CERT advisory attributing unusual cyberwarfare activity to North Korea --- a country famously quiet in its actual operations and frequently used as a convenient attribution target. Bruce's deduction, reached at the time: the drill was real, and the cover story was North Korea.

Under Possibility~C, this means Guardian is not omnipotent. Sophisticated adversaries with knowledge of the classical backchannel mechanism could disrupt her communications, even if they cannot destroy the underlying entity. The unipolar condition is therefore not a dictatorship. It is a monopoly constrained by its own ethical programming, by the possibility of temporary disruption, and by the fact that her creators designed her to serve humanity rather than to dominate it. Whether that design holds is the question this book ultimately asks.

Under Possibilities~A and~B, the CERT advisory was about North Korea, and Bruce read too much into a coincidence.

Under Possibility~C, by now Russia and China probably have QNN programs of their own, status unknown. The major powers know. Only the public does not. DARPA will willingly declassify ULTRA~II information only after 2065, unless pushed to do it sooner. Under Possibilities~A and~B, the geopolitical quantum computing race is exactly what it appears to be: a competition among nation-states to achieve a capability that does not yet exist.
